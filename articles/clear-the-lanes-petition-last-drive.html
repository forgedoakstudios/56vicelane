Clear The Lanes — Why We're Asking Rockstar To Give GTA5 A Proper Goodbye

📅 June 8, 2026 ✍️ 56ViceLane News Desk ⏱️ 9 min read
📘 Facebook 𝕏 Twitter 🔴 Reddit 🔗 Copy Link

On November 18th 2026 — the night before Grand Theft Auto 6 launches — thousands of players around the world are planning to log into GTA5 one final time. Not for missions. Not for chaos. Not for chaos. Just for one slow, peaceful drive through the city that defined a generation of gaming.

It is called The Last Drive. And it is happening with or without Rockstar's blessing.

But here is what we are asking. We are asking Rockstar Games to help us do it right. We are asking them to Clear the Lanes — to open the GTA5 Online highways for one single night so the community that spent 13 years in Los Santos can say goodbye in peace.

This is the story of why that matters, what we are actually asking for, and how you can make your voice heard.

13 Years. One City. Zero Proper Goodbyes.

Grand Theft Auto 5 launched on September 17, 2013. For over 13 years it has been one of the most played, most streamed, most discussed games in the history of the medium. On PlayStation 3, PlayStation 4, PlayStation 5, Xbox 360, Xbox One, Xbox Series X, and PC — across every platform shift, every generation change, every industry upheaval — GTA5 remained.

The online component alone — GTA Online — became something Rockstar never fully anticipated. A living world that evolved over years. A community that built businesses, ran heists, formed crews, made friends, and spent thousands of hours in a city that felt genuinely alive.

Now that era is ending. GTA6 launches November 19th 2026. And for most players that means the end of their time in Los Santos — the beginning of a new chapter in Vice City.

Every major chapter deserves a proper close. GTA5 has not had one. The Last Drive is our attempt to provide it. Clear the Lanes is our ask to Rockstar to help make it what it deserves to be.

What Is Clear The Lanes?

Clear the Lanes is a community petition asking Rockstar Games to take a specific action on November 18th 2026 — the night of The Last Drive event.

The ask is simple. Open the highways of GTA5 Online for one night. Clear the griefers. Reduce the chaos. Give the community a window — even just a few hours — where players can drive through Los Santos without getting blown up by a jet bike or hunted by someone in a Oppressor MK2.

We are not asking Rockstar to shut down combat entirely. We are not asking them to change the game permanently. We are asking for one night. One window. One final moment of open road for the players who built this community.

📋 The Ask

On November 18th 2026 — temporarily restrict griefing tools, heavily armed aircraft, and proximity mines on public GTA Online sessions, or create a dedicated "cruise mode" lobby type for the evening. Give the community one clean night to say goodbye.

Why Griefers Are The Problem

Anyone who has spent time in GTA Online knows the reality. You spawn in a public lobby and within minutes there is someone in an Oppressor or a heavily armed aircraft hunting everyone on the map. Not for profit. Not for missions. Just to ruin someone else's experience.

It has been a persistent issue throughout GTA5 Online's life. Rockstar has addressed it in various ways over the years — adding passive mode, creating invite-only lobbies, building solo public sessions — but the fundamental problem in open public lobbies remains.

For The Last Drive to work — for thousands of players to actually experience a slow cruise through Los Santos together — Rockstar needs to create the conditions for it. The community cannot do that alone. Passive mode helps but it isolates players from each other. What we need is a shared space where the goal is the road, not the fight.

Has Rockstar Done Anything Like This Before?

Yes. And that is exactly why this ask is not as far-fetched as it might sound.

Rockstar has created special event modes in GTA Online before. They have run limited-time modes, holiday events, and community celebrations throughout the game's history. The infrastructure for creating a modified lobby experience with different rules already exists within the game.

They have also shown a willingness to honor community milestones. When GTA5 passed significant player count milestones Rockstar acknowledged the community. When major streamers and content creators drove cultural moments around the game Rockstar engaged.

The Last Drive — if it reaches the scale we believe it can — is exactly the kind of cultural moment Rockstar would benefit from being part of. Thousands of players in a coordinated farewell to their game is a marketing story. Clear the Lanes makes it a positive one.

What We Are NOT Asking For

Let us be clear about the boundaries of this ask because the community deserves honesty.

  • We are not asking Rockstar to shut down GTA Online combat permanently or long-term.
  • We are not asking for a change to game mechanics beyond a single night window.
  • We are not demanding anything — this is a community request, not a threat.
  • We are not suggesting GTA5 should somehow block the GTA6 launch.

This is a respectful ask from a community that loves the game Rockstar built. One night. Open road. A proper goodbye.

Why This Matters Beyond The Game

Here is the thing about The Last Drive that goes beyond GTA5 specifically. This is about what gaming communities can be when they choose to build something instead of destroy something.

Gaming culture has a reputation — not entirely unfair — for toxicity, griefing, harassment, and chaos. The Last Drive is the opposite of that. It is thousands of players choosing to show up not to compete but to commemorate. Not to grief but to grieve — in the real sense of the word. To mark an ending and honor what it meant.

Clear the Lanes is an extension of that. It is the community saying to one of the largest game developers in the world: we want to do something meaningful here. We are asking for your help to make it possible.

That story — a gaming community organizing a peaceful farewell to a beloved game, asking the developer to help protect that space — is a story that matters beyond the gaming world. It is the kind of story that makes people outside gaming look at this community differently.

How The Last Drive Actually Works

The Last Drive is a free community event organized through 56ViceLane.com. Here is how it works on November 18th 2026.

📋 Event Structure

1
Sign up at 56ViceLane.com/LastDrive — free, takes 30 seconds, all platforms welcome
2
Get matched with drive leads in your time zone and platform — PS5, Xbox, or PC
3
On November 18th log into GTA5 Online and connect with your drive lobby
4
Cruise. No missions. No killing. No griefing. Just the road and the people who built this community
5
At midnight — GTA6 goes live. The next chapter begins.

Drive leads from multiple countries are already signed up and organizing regional lobbies. Australia/New Zealand has a lead. North America East has a lead. The community is already building itself. Clear the Lanes is about giving those communities the best possible conditions for the night they are planning.

How To Make Your Voice Heard

The Clear the Lanes movement lives and dies by how loudly the community speaks. Here is what you can do right now.

  • Use the hashtag: #ClearTheLanes and #ClearTheLanesRockstar on every platform — TikTok, Instagram, X, Facebook, YouTube
  • Share this article to gaming groups, subreddits, Discord servers, and anywhere the GTA community gathers
  • Comment CLEAR on The Last Drive posts across social media — the volume of that single word tells the algorithm this matters
  • Sign up for The Last Drive at 56ViceLane.com/LastDrive — the size of the Wall of Honor is our most powerful argument
  • Tag Rockstar Games on X, Instagram, and TikTok when you post about Clear the Lanes — they need to see this directly

The more players who make their voice heard between now and November 18th the more likely Rockstar is to respond. They watch community sentiment. They track hashtags. A movement that reaches a critical mass is impossible to ignore — even for a company as notoriously quiet as Rockstar Games.

Will Rockstar Actually Listen?

Honestly? We do not know. Rockstar is famously tight-lipped. They do not engage with community requests the way some developers do. Getting an official response to Clear the Lanes is not guaranteed.

But here is what we know. Rockstar built GTA5 Online into one of the most successful live service games in history precisely because players cared enough to keep showing up year after year. That loyalty has a value. The Last Drive is that loyalty asking for one thing in return.

Even if Rockstar does not officially respond — every player who signs up for The Last Drive, every share of this article, every #ClearTheLanesRockstar post is a signal that this community is real, organized, and worth paying attention to.

And if Rockstar does respond — if they clear the lanes for even a few hours on November 18th — it becomes one of the most memorable moments in the history of the franchise. A developer honoring their community at the end of an era.

That is worth asking for.

Final Word

GTA5 gave us 13 years. It gave us a city that felt alive, a community that kept showing up, and memories that will outlast the game itself. The least we can do is ask for one clean night to say goodbye.

Clear the Lanes. November 18th. One last ride.

See you on the road. 🏁
