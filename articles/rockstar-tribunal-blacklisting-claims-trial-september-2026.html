Tribunal Rules Against Rockstar — Blacklisting Claims Stay, Full Trial Starts September 10

📅 June 17, 2026 ✍️ 56ViceLane News Desk ⏱️ 8 min read
📘 Facebook 𝕏 Twitter 🔴 Reddit 🔗 Copy Link

A UK employment tribunal has handed Rockstar Games a significant legal setback, rejecting the studio's attempt to strip blacklisting allegations from the ongoing union-busting case brought by the Independent Workers' Union of Great Britain. Every allegation now proceeds to full trial. The trial begins September 10, 2026 and runs through October 15 — with GTA6 launching just five weeks later on November 19.

Thirty-one workers. All of them union members. All fired in October 2025 in what the IWGB called "one of the most blatant and ruthless acts of union busting in the history of the games industry." Rockstar Games said the dismissals were for gross misconduct. A court is now going to hear both sides of that story — in full, with nothing removed.

⚖️ What Just Happened

At a recent preliminary hearing, Rockstar Games asked the tribunal to remove blacklisting claims from the case before it went to full trial. The tribunal refused. Blacklisting — the prohibited practice of listing trade union members for use in hiring or management decisions — will now be among the allegations tested in open court starting September 10.

What Is Blacklisting — And Why Does It Matter?

Blacklisting in the employment context refers to the prohibited practice of compiling lists of union members, activists, or workers who've raised health and safety concerns, and using those lists to make recruitment or disciplinary decisions. In the UK it is illegal under the Employment Relations Act and the Blacklisting Regulations 2010. It is not a minor procedural complaint — it is one of the most serious allegations that can be brought against an employer in a labor dispute.

The IWGB's claim is that the 31 workers weren't fired for gross misconduct as Rockstar Games maintains — but that they were identified as union members and targeted for that reason. The blacklisting allegation, specifically, goes a step further: it suggests there was a list. That the identification was systematic. The tribunal ruling means the evidence for that claim now gets tested in open court, in front of a judge, with both sides required to produce documentation and witnesses.

Rockstar Games wanted that allegation removed before the trial even began. The tribunal said no.

What the Workers Are Saying

Ellie Dunstan, one of the 31 workers fired by Rockstar last October, responded to today's ruling directly. "Rockstar thought they could control the narrative. They're wrong, and we look forward to proving it. Our case will now be heard in full and put to the test as it should be. The world will get to see for itself the evidence as to what happened last October. We loved our work at Rockstar. Losing our passion, our colleagues and our incomes in the blink of an eye was devastating, and the company management has treated us with disdain ever since, refusing to grant us appeals or respond to basic evidence requests."

IWGB Game Workers Branch chair Spring McParlin-Jones described today's ruling as "a major blow to Rockstar's attempts to avoid scrutiny for its actions." McParlin-Jones said the tribunal found "serious factual questions remain about how these workers were identified, listed and dismissed — questions that must now be tested at a full hearing." She added: "From the moment employees were escorted from their buildings without warning, and throughout every subsequent stage of the legal proceedings, Rockstar has attempted to avoid accountability."

ℹ️ Rockstar's Position

Rockstar Games has maintained the 31 workers were dismissed for gross misconduct, not union membership. The company has not publicly responded to the blacklisting allegations specifically, nor to today's tribunal ruling. Eurogamer reports they requested comment and had not received a response at time of publication. Both sides will present their full cases at the September trial.

The Timing Nobody Is Talking About

Here is the part of this story that deserves more attention than it's getting. The trial runs September 10 through October 15, 2026. GTA6 launches November 19, 2026. That means this case will be heard, argued, and concluded — with findings and rulings — in the five weeks immediately before the biggest game launch in history.

If the tribunal finds against Rockstar Games, those findings land in mid-October. The game launches a month later. The marketing campaign — Trailer 3, pre-orders, the full promotional push — will be running simultaneously with trial coverage. That is an extraordinary overlap for a company trying to position a $2 billion launch as a cultural celebration.

It also puts the gaming press in an unusual position. The same outlets covering GTA6 hype will be covering trial developments. The same community that's hyped for November 19 includes the people who signed that 200-person letter condemning the firings. This is not a story that goes away quietly — and today's ruling made sure of it.

What It Means for the People Who Built the Game

It's worth stepping back from the legal mechanics for a moment and acknowledging what's actually at stake here on a human level. Thirty-one people — developers, artists, designers, engineers — who spent years working on GTA6 were walked out of their building in October 2025. They lost their jobs, their colleagues, and their income overnight. Many of them have been fighting this case for eight months with no appeals granted and no responses to evidence requests.

The game they helped build is five months from launching to a global audience. They will not be there for it. Their names may or may not appear in the credits. And the company they worked for tried to have the most serious allegations against it removed from court before anyone could hear them.

The tribunal said no. Their case gets heard. Whether the outcome is what they're hoping for remains to be seen — but the legal process is at least going to run its full course.

Where This Goes From Here

The trial itself runs five weeks. Employment tribunals in the UK produce written judgments — they don't hand down verdicts from the bench at the end of proceedings. A written ruling could arrive weeks or even months after October 15, which means the final legal outcome may not land until well after GTA6 has launched.

But the testimony, the evidence, and the public record of what both sides argue will be visible in September and October. Employment tribunal proceedings in the UK are generally public. That means whatever Rockstar Games' internal documents show about how those 31 workers were identified and dismissed is going to enter the public record during the heart of GTA6's launch window.

McParlin-Jones put it plainly: "This David versus Goliath legal battle" is entering its final stage. A tribunal has already found there are "serious factual questions" about how the workers were identified, listed, and dismissed. Those questions now get answered in open court.
